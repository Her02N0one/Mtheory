The CAGED System

Every guitarist learns five open-chord shapes: C, A, G, E, D. Each of those shapes can be moved up the neck with a barre — the notes change, the fingering geometry stays the same. Slide an E-chord shape up 3 frets and you have a G chord. Slide a D shape up 2 frets and you have an E chord.

These five shapes tile the neck end-to-end without gaps. In any key, all five appear in sequence as you climb from nut to body — then the cycle repeats an octave higher. Learning all five boxes gives you full-neck coverage in any key, and every note you'll ever play belongs to one of the five shapes you already know.

The pentatonic scale maps cleanly onto this system: each shape produces a compact two-notes-per-string box. Five shapes × 5 notes = a complete pentatonic system from open strings to the 12th fret.

Neck order (ascending): C → A → G → E → D → C → ···

Major & Relative Minor

Every major key has a relative minor that shares all the same notes. C major and A minor use identical pitches — the scales differ only in which note is the tonal centre. On the fretboard this means the five box positions are identical; only the root dots shift.

Practicing both perspectives in each box trains your ear to hear the same shapes as either bright (major) or dark (minor). One set of shapes, two tonal colours — one of the most efficient uses of practice time on the guitar.

The three key groups here — C/Am, G/Em, F/Dm — cover the most common open-position keys and introduce every natural note on the neck before any accidentals.

The B-String Rule

Every adjacent pair of strings in standard tuning is a perfect 4th (5 semitones) apart: E→A, A→D, D→G, B→e. Every pair — except one. The G string to B string gap is only a major 3rd (4 semitones). One semitone short.

This means any scale shape that crosses from the G string to the B string must shift 1 fret higher on the B and high-e strings. Ignore the shift and the notes are wrong.

The five CAGED shapes split into two groups based on this gap. The E and A shapes anchor their root on the bass strings (6th and 5th) — the B-string crossing is far from the root. The G, C, and D shapes have their root on the G, D, or B string itself — the scale immediately runs into or starts at the anomalous gap.

G-shape root is on the G string: the very next string up is B — the gap is the first thing you cross. D-shape root is on the D string: two strings away from B, with G in between. C-shape root lands directly on the B string.

Vocabulary

Pentatonic scale
A 5-note scale. Major pentatonic removes the 4th and 7th from a major scale; minor pentatonic removes the 2nd and 6th from natural minor.
CAGED system
Five movable scale/chord shapes — C, A, G, E, D — that tile the fretboard end-to-end. Any note on the neck belongs to one of the five shapes.
Box position
A compact 3–5 fret window containing two scale notes per string. Five boxes give complete neck coverage.
Root note
The tonal centre of the scale. Root dots are the anchor you orient each box around — the note the scale wants to resolve to.
Relative minor
A minor scale sharing every note with a major scale. A minor is relative to C major — same boxes, different root emphasis and emotional colour.
Parallel minor
A minor scale with the same root but different notes. C minor is parallel to C major — different shapes, same tonic.
Open position
A box that includes fret 0 (open strings). Shapes at the nut end of the neck sit in open position.
B-string shift
The 1-fret upward adjustment on the B and high-E strings, caused by the G→B interval being a major 3rd rather than a perfect 4th.
