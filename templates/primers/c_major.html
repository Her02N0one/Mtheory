Seven degrees — two half steps added back

The major scale adds degrees 4 (F) and 7 (B) back to the pentatonic. These are the two notes that were removed to make the pentatonic "safe." Now they’re back — and they carry tension.

The shape law — applied to scale degrees

Look at the token shapes above. This is not random:

  • Degrees 1 2 3 are all ◼ squares — the same shape as the root.
  • Degrees 4 5 6 7 are all ● circles — the other shape.

This is the shape law in action. Degrees 1, 2, 3 are 0, 2, and 4 semitones from the root — all even, so same shape. Degrees 4, 5, 6, 7 are 5, 7, 9, 11 semitones from the root — all odd, so different shape. The rule holds in every key without exception.

The half steps are the shape boundaries

The two shape crossings in the scale are exactly where the half steps live:

  • 3→4   E → F  ◼→●   half step (odd: 1 semitone)
  • 7→1   B → C  ●→◼   half step (odd: 1 semitone)

These are the only two places in the major scale where adjacent notes have different shapes. Every other adjacent pair is whole-step (same shape). On the neck they appear as two frets with no gap — feel them and the pattern locks in.

Navigation principle

From any note on the neck:

  • To reach same-shape degrees (1→2, 1→3, 2→3, 5→6, 4→5, 4→6, 4→7…): stack whole steps only — no half step needed.
  • To reach other-shape degrees (1→4, 1→5, 3→4, 3→7…): your path crosses one shape boundary — one half step is built in.

The leading tone (7→1) and subdominant fall (4→3) are gravitational because they are the two shortest shape-boundary crossings: one half step, one fret, the smallest possible move across the divide.

Lesson structure

  • Open box — Core. Lock the seven degrees in position.
  • Mid box — Core. Moveable. Drill the intervals between degrees.
  • Upper box — Core. Add diatonic chord shapes.
  • ⚡ Connect + Full neck — Challenge. Free recall; the shape law is your guide.
