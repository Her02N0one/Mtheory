Five scale degrees

The Nashville Number System names notes by their position in the scale, not their letter name. These five notes are always 1 2 3 5 6 — in any key, the pentatonic skips 4 and 7.

W = whole step (2 frets). m3 = minor third (3 frets — the skip where 4 and 7 used to be).

The shape law

Every note in this system has a shape: ◼ square or ● circle. The rule is absolute:

  • Same shape → even number of semitones apart (0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12…)
  • Different shape → odd number of semitones apart (1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11)

That means: to reach a note of the same shape, you can always get there by stacking whole steps only. To reach a note of the other shape, your path must include exactly one half step somewhere.

Two camps in the pentatonic

Notice the tokens above: degrees 1 2 3 are all ◼ squares. Degrees 5 6 are both ● circles. This is not a coincidence — the pentatonic removes the two notes (4 = F and 7 = B) that would otherwise sit right at the shape boundaries as half-step neighbors.

Within each camp you only ever travel by whole steps. Crossing between camps (3→5, minor third) costs exactly one "odd" interval — the shape changes once.

Three positions on the neck

  • Open box (frets 0–4) — lock the shape: 1 2 3 on one side, 5 6 on the other.
  • Mid box (frets 5–9) — same five degrees, fully closed and moveable to any key.
  • Upper box (frets 9–13) — same degrees, one octave higher.

The ⚡ Challenge phases connect these boxes — the shape law is your map for navigating between them.
