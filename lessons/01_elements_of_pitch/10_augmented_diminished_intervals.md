# Augmented & Diminished Intervals

Beyond major, minor, and perfect lie two more qualities. **Augmented** is one semitone *larger* than major or perfect. **Diminished** is one semitone *smaller* than minor or perfect.

## The rule

| Starting quality | Raise 1 semitone | Lower 1 semitone |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Perfect | Augmented | Diminished |
| Major | Augmented | Minor |
| Minor | Major | Diminished |

:::callout key
You can think of it as a chain:  
**Diminished — Minor — Major — Augmented** (for 2nds, 3rds, 6ths, 7ths)  
**Diminished — Perfect — Augmented** (for Unison, 4ths, 5ths, Octaves)
:::

## The tritone

The most important augmented/diminished interval is the **tritone** — exactly six semitones, the dead centre of the octave.

It has two enharmonic spellings:
- **Augmented 4th** (A4): C–F♯ (four letters, one semitone wider than P4)
- **Diminished 5th** (d5): C–G♭ (five letters, one semitone narrower than P5)

Both sound identical on the guitar. The spelling depends on the musical context.

:::callout info
The tritone is the only interval that divides the octave exactly in half. It sounds maximally tense and unstable — its resolution is the engine of tonal harmony. In dominant 7th chords the tritone between the 3rd and 7th is what creates the strong pull toward the tonic.
:::

## Augmented 2nd — harmonic minor's signature

You already heard this in the harmonic minor scale: the gap between ♭6̂ and 7̂ is an **augmented 2nd** (A2) — three semitones wide, same as a minor 3rd, but spelled as a second because adjacent letter names are involved.

| Interval | Semitones | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Augmented 2nd (A2) | 3 | A♭–B (c harmonic minor, degrees 6–7) |
| Diminished 5th (d5) | 6 | B–F (C major, degrees 7–4) |
| Augmented 4th (A4) | 6 | F–B (C major, degrees 4–7) |
| Diminished 7th (d7) | 9 | B–A♭ (c harmonic minor, degrees 7–♭6 one octave up) |

## On the neck: the tritone shape

Two common tritone shapes on the guitar:

- **Same string:** 6 frets up
- **Adjacent strings (E–A, A–D, D–G):** 1 fret up on the higher string
- **G–B pair:** same fret (because G–B is only a major 3rd apart)

The tritone is the key to the "substitution" sound in jazz and the screaming tension in metal — knowing its shape gives you direct access to that sound.
