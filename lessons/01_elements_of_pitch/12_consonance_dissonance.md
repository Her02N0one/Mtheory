# Consonant & Dissonant Intervals

Not all intervals are equal in stability. **Consonant** intervals sound at rest; **dissonant** intervals create tension that demands resolution. This distinction is the engine of melody and harmony — tension and release, question and answer.

## The spectrum

Intervals are traditionally classified from most stable to most tense:

| Interval | Semitones | Category |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Perfect Unison (P1) | 0 | **Perfect consonance** |
| Perfect Octave (P8) | 12 | **Perfect consonance** |
| Perfect 5th (P5) | 7 | **Perfect consonance** |
| Perfect 4th (P4) | 5 | **Imperfect consonance** (context-dependent) |
| Major 3rd (M3) | 4 | **Imperfect consonance** |
| Minor 3rd (m3) | 3 | **Imperfect consonance** |
| Major 6th (M6) | 9 | **Imperfect consonance** |
| Minor 6th (m6) | 8 | **Imperfect consonance** |
| Major 2nd (M2) | 2 | **Mild dissonance** |
| Minor 7th (m7) | 10 | **Mild dissonance** |
| Major 7th (M7) | 11 | **Strong dissonance** |
| Minor 2nd (m2) | 1 | **Strong dissonance** |
| Tritone (A4/d5) | 6 | **Maximum dissonance** |

:::callout info
The **Perfect 4th** is a borderline case. In two-voice counterpoint it is dissonant (it needs to resolve). In chords with a bass note below it, it is consonant. Context matters.
:::

## Consonance and the overtone series

The consonant intervals correspond closely to the **harmonic overtone series** — the natural resonances of a vibrating string. The simpler the frequency ratio, the more consonant the interval:

- Perfect Unison: 1:1
- Perfect Octave: 2:1
- Perfect 5th: 3:2
- Perfect 4th: 4:3
- Major 3rd: 5:4
- Minor 3rd: 6:5

Dissonant intervals have more complex ratios. The tritone (45:32 or 64:45) is the furthest from a simple ratio, which is why it sounds maximally tense.

## On the guitar: using consonance and dissonance

**Power chords** (root + P5) use only perfect consonances — maximum stability, zero colour.

**Major chords** add the M3 — now the sound has brightness and warmth (imperfect consonance).

**Minor chords** use the m3 instead — darker, more serious.

**Dominant 7th chords** (root, M3, P5, m7) add a mild dissonance (m7) on top — this creates the pull toward resolution that makes them so effective at the V chord position.

**The tritone** in a dominant 7th (between the M3 and m7) is the sharpest dissonance in common use — it demands resolution and is the single most powerful harmonic device in tonal music.

:::widget scaleview {view: "keyboard", root: "C4", scale: "major", steps: true, labels: "degrees", interactive: true}
:::

Play and listen: C and G (P5) — open and stable. C and B (M7) — bright tension. C and D♭ (m2) — maximum crunch. Train your ear to hear where each interval sits on the spectrum.
